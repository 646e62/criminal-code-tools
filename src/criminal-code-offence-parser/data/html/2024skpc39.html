Date:               November 6, 2024 

Information:   991252530

Location:         Saskatoon

_____________________________________________________________________________

 

Between:

 

His Majesty the King

 

- and -

 

Laird Johnston

 

Appearing:

 

B. Rogers                                                                                                                     For the Crown

L. Marchand                                                                                                            For the Accused

                                                                                                                                                           

 

SENTENCING DECISION                                                                        D.W. HOWARTH, J

                                                                                                                                                           

 

 

[1]               Laird Johnston (Mr. Johnston) was convicted after trial of possession of fentanyl, methamphetamine, GHB, and cocaine for the purposes of trafficking under section 5(2) of the CDSA.[1] He was also convicted of section 95 and section 94 offences in relation to a prohibited firearm in the trunk of the vehicle he was driving. He was also found guilty of breaching two firearms prohibition orders under section 117.01 of the Criminal Code and plead guilty to driving while prohibited contrary to section 320.18(1)(a) of the Criminal Code.

 

BACKGROUND

[2]               The charges arise out of a vehicle stop and subsequent search that occurred in the late evening of July 23, 2023.  Two University of Saskatchewan Protective Services (USPS) peace officers where on regular patrol in a marked police vehicle when they observed a black Chrysler driving near the University of Saskatchewan. Upon running the licence plate, they found that the vehicle was registered to 23-year-old female (Wyntre Sylvestre) who was a suspended driver. Therefore, they initiated a traffic stop to check for valid driver’s licence and registration.

 

[3]               The driver of the vehicle turned out to be 38-year-old (at the time) Mr. Johnston.  The only other occupant of the vehicle was a male sitting in the front passenger seat. That individual was 56-year-old Mr. Campodonico.

 

[4]               Cst. Huebner of the USPS, after interacting with Mr. Johnston, found that he did not have a valid driver’s licence.  She also suspected he was impaired by drug and made a demand for him to provide a sample into a roadside screening device.  He was asked to exit the vehicle for that purpose.  The USPS officers both noted that it seemed odd that there was a green fanny pack lying between Mr. Johnston’s feet, since he was the driver of the vehicle.  Shortly thereafter it was discovered that he was a disqualified driver, and then later a Canada wide criminally prohibited driver. He was arrested for those offences.  On search incident to arrest, a bag of cocaine was located in his front pocket.

 

[5]               Once the cocaine was located in his pocket he was arrested for that offence, and Cpl. Tahn of the USPS searched the driver’s seat of the vehicle, in a search incident to arrest, for further evidence of the offence.  On searching of the fanny pack that had been at Mr. Johnston’s feet Cpl. Tahn found bags containing a significant amount of various controlled substances. At that point USPS suspected drug trafficking and Saskatoon Police Service (SPS) was called to take over the investigation.  Cpl. Tahn also located rifle magazines in the front driver’s side door well, one of which was loaded with .22 ammunition.

 

[6]               The USPS officers learned that Mr. Campodonico, the passenger, had a warrant out for his arrest on bylaw matters, and he was then arrested on that warrant.  On search incident to arrest, a large bundle of cash was located in his pocket.

 

[7]               When SPS officers arrived, they formed grounds to arrest Mr. Campodonico and Mr. Johnston for trafficking in a controlled substance, and the rest of the vehicle was searched incident to arrest.  There were numerous weapons, ammunition, cellphones, and drug paraphernalia located in the vehicle.  Notably, a prohibited firearm, being a .22 semi-automatic rifle (which has the appearance of an assault style rifle) was located in the spare tire compartment of the trunk. 

 

[8]               The illegal drugs located in the vehicle, or on Mr. Johnston’s person, were as follows:

 

a.                  One bag containing 39.7 grams of cocaine located in the green fanny pack;

 

b.                  One bag containing 13.35 grams of fentanyl mixture located in the green fanny    pack;

 

c.                  One bag containing 8.06 grams of cocaine located in Mr. Johnston’s left pocket;

 

d.                  One bag containing 114.1 grams of methamphetamine located in the green fanny pack;

 

e.                  One bag containing 3.99 grams of MDMA located in centre console of vehicle;

 

f.                    Two bags containing a total of 9 hydromorphone pills located in the green fanny   pack;

 

g.                  One container containing 34 hydromorphone pills located in a backpack in the      trunk;

 

h.                  Two drink bottles, containing 227.35 grams and 250.35 grams respectively, of       GHB located             in the green fanny pack.

 

POSITIONS OF THE PARTIES

[9]               The Crown and Mr. Johnston differ significantly on what they submit the most appropriate sentence should be.

 

[10]           The Crown submits that the appropriate sentence for Mr. Johnston, given his record and the nature of the offence, is 18 years imprisonment, reduced to 14 years after totality is considered.

 

[11]           Mr. Johnston, through his counsel, submits the appropriate sentence, for the combined offences, is a global sentence of 8 years jail, reduced to 6 years after considering the principal of totality.

 

[12]           There is no dispute on the ancillary orders sought by the Crown.

 

REVIEW OF THE CASE LAW

[13]           The Crown provided me with 20 cases they wished me to consider for particular sentencing principals and as appropriate comparators when determining the appropriate sentence. Mr. Johnston submitted that many of the cases were distinguishable and so not necessarily appropriate comparators.  He further argues that the Crown has not proven he was anything more than a low-level or street level dealer, and that he does not have the prior violent or significant drug trafficking history of other offenders in some of the cases referred to by the Crown. I will go through the cases filed by the Crown in the order that they were filed.

 

[14]           The first case filed was that of R v Ratt, 2021 SKCA 7 [Ratt].  This was a case of an offender convicted after trial of aggravated assault, obstruction and two breaches. Mr. Ratt had significant Gladue[2] factors.  Mr. Ratt had been in foster care from a young age, had experienced trauma at a young age, and had early abuse of alcohol.  Mr. Ratt also had a lengthy and violent criminal record and had a history of noncompliance. The Crown argued that the principle of restraint, with respect to Indigenous offenders, should play a lesser role under those circumstances and noted that he had done nothing to address his risk factors despite indications in previous sentencings that he would do so.  The Crown had asked for 7 years jail, and the trial judge imposed a global sentence of 3 years and 10 months. The Court of Appeal overturned the sentence and imposed a sentence of 5.5 years jail. In doing so the Court held at paragraph 55 that “the Crown is correct to assert that the situation cried out for a sentence that puts protection of the public at the forefront of the relevant sentencing objectives.”

 

[15]           The Court went on to note the following at paragraph 61:

 

[61]      In R v Khawaja, 2012 SCC 69, [2012] 3 SCR 555 [Khawaja], the Supreme Court of Canada agreed that the absence of rehabilitative prospects is an important factor to be considered in sentencing, “particularly in regard to s. 718(c) and the need to separate offenders from society, where necessary” (at para 122). On that point, McLachlin C.J.C., writing for the Court, cited the following passage from R v Downey,  2010 ONSC 1531[Downey], with approval:

Where it is apparent that the offender is a dangerous person, who is likely to compromise public safety if released, he should be detained for a period of time sufficient to reasonably conclude that such danger has subsided. The duration of the sentence must be sufficient to give the correctional authorities the necessary time to properly treat the offender and for the National Parole Board to assess the risk of his reoffending.

  

[16]           The second case is that of R v Van Der Haak, 2018 ABCA 352.  In that case the accused was sentenced to 3 years and 9 months for trafficking in 26.3 grams of cocaine, 7.6 grams of meth, as well as marijuana, GHB, codeine pills and a section 117 weapons prohibition violation. The value of drugs was approximately $3,500.00.  Noteworthy is that he had a prior 2012 conviction for trafficking in multiple drugs.  The Court of Appeal upheld the sentence noting in paragraph 15 that, while the offenders’ efforts at rehabilitation and were significant, and he had been previously gainfully employed, that cannot outweigh the need for denunciation and deterrence in these circumstances.

 

[17]           The third case is that of R v Chin, 2009 ABCA 226, 457 AR 233 [Chin].  Mr. Chin was found guilty after trial of possessing 15 grams of cocaine for the purpose of trafficking along with a loaded handgun, $3,800.00 cash and a bulletproof vest.  He was 22 years old with a relatively minor criminal record.  The Court of Appeal upheld the global sentence of 5 years jail.  In doing so the Alberta Court of Appeal went to note (in paragraphs 6 through 12) the terrible cost to society do to drug addiction and trafficking, specifically in cocaine and methamphetamine. It described it as a “scourge on society” noted not only that trafficking in such drugs lead to the terrible loss of productive citizens through addiction, but also involves tremendous loss due to crimes committed incidental to the trafficking of drugs such as robberies and theft committed for addicts to get money for drugs.  It further noted that people are lured into drug trafficking by the promise of quick and easy money and that sentences must be sufficient enough such that they are a real deterrent and not simply seen as a cost of doing business. 

 

[18]           The Court in Chin further noted the problem of guns combined with drugs and noted that sentences for those offences must also be sufficient to deter criminals from arming themselves with firearms (in that case handguns).  I agree with those statements.

 

[19]           The fourth case was from the Supreme Court of Canada: R v Nur, 2015 SCC 15, [2015] 1 SCR 773 [Nur].  Nur was found guilty of possessing a loaded handgun with an oversized ammunition clip outside a community centre.  The gun was a prohibited firearm.  Nur had no criminal record and many positive, community supports.  The Supreme Court, while overturning the 3-year mandatory minimum sentence for section 95, upheld the 3-year sentence for Mr. Nur, as the situation in that case was more than a mere licensing or handling violation of a lawful gun owner. At paragraph 82 the Supreme Court noted that sentences of 3 years or more will continue to be appropriate when someone possesses a loaded restricted or prohibited firearm as a tool of his or her criminal trade.

 

[20]           The fifth case was R v Bedi, 2015 ABCA 361, 609 AR 74 [Bedi].  In Bedi the Alberta Court of Appeal did not overturn a sentence of 8 years imposed by a trial judge.  Mr. Bedi had plead guilty to 2 section 95 offences (for a Glock handgun and a sawed of rifle) and two possession for purpose of trafficking (PPT) offences (for 300 grams of heroin and 99 grams of crack cocaine) as well a number of breaches.  Mr. Bedi had good family support and he had a minimal criminal record (that was his first custodial offence).  

 

[21]           I note that the Bedi case involves a somewhat greater quantity of hard drugs as well as two firearms, both of which were very dangerous, being a handgun and a sawed-off shotgun. Those factors may be a little more aggravating than in the current circumstances, and there seems to be no mention of Gladue factors in Bedi. However, Mr. Johnston has a much more significant and aggravating criminal record and does not have the benefit of guilty pleas. Balancing those competing factors out, the case is somewhat of a good comparator.

 

[22]           The sixth case is R v Kamara, 2020 SKCA 76 [Kamara].  Mr. Kamara was found guilty after trial of possessing 190 grams of cocaine for the purposes of trafficking as well as a section 95 offence in relation to a loaded 9mm handgun and proceeds of crime of over $13,000.00 cash as well as a number of more minor connected offences.  His prior record included only a single section 4(1) possession and single section 145 breach offence.  He was sentenced by the trial judge to 6.5 years (3.5 for the PPT and 3 years consecutive for the firearms offences).  Notably, the trial judge found Mr. Kamara to be a “significant player” in the drug trafficking operation, a mid-level dealer who supplied street level dealers, was motivated by only profit, and had a stash house where he cooked the cocaine. The Court of Appeal upheld the sentence, noting it was “heavy but not demonstrably unfit”.

 

[23]           Kamara is a good comparator, but in the case before the Court, the Crown has not proven that Mr. Johnston was a “significant player” or mid-level dealer in charge of a significant operation or proceeds.  Further, I find that Mr. Johnston was a meth addict and was dealing drugs, in part, to support his habit.  On the other hand, it must be noted that Mr. Kamara had nothing in the way of a prior record compared to Mr. Johnston.

 

[24]           The seventh case is R v Marshall, 2015 ONCA 692.  Mr. Marshall was convicted after trial of possessing a loaded prohibited handgun at the time he was trafficking drugs.  He plead guilty to subsequent trafficking offences and was sentenced to 3.5 years for the section 95 offence and 1 year consecutive for the trafficking offence.  He was a youthful first-time offender but had a negative pre-sentence report (PSR) where he rationalized the offences and stated that he saw no need to make changes in his life.  Mr. Marshall appealed the sentence on the section 95 offence.  The Court of Appeal upheld the sentence noting it was well within the range as it was a section 95 offence committed in association with drug crimes.

 

[25]           The eighth case is the more recent case of R v Wisdom, 2024 ONSC 4047 [Wisdom].  The Crown relies on this case from the Ontario Superior Court as setting out a 6 to 9 years range for section 95 recidivism (see paragraph 56) and that Ontario has a range of 7 to 10 years for “mid- level” commercial fentanyl trafficking. All the details of the two co-accused in Wisdom are difficult to summarize quickly.  The offender on which the Crown relies on for parity was Jason Wisdom. He was convicted after trial with possession for the purpose of trafficking of 356.88 grams of fentanyl, 174.29 grams of cocaine (powder and crack), 330.91 grams of phenacetin cutting agent, 28.02 grams of methamphetamine and of $4,460.00 cash proceeds of crime. The Court noted the quantities and varieties possessed by Jason were “extraordinary”.  He had a sophisticated operation with digital scales, vacuum sealer and a loaded restricted handgun found with the drugs with a round in the chamber, and a 14 round overcapacity magazine (which in totality is more aggravating than the situation before this Court).  It was also quite aggravating that there was a small child present in the house that could have been endangered by the guns and drugs.  Further, he was found on the evidence to be supplying other dealers and there was no suggestion he was a user or addict of the drugs. He was found to be “in close connection to high-level wholesale distributors of fentanyl and he was capable of multi-ounce wholesale distribution of fentanyl.”  I note that the quantity of drugs and level of operation is far more significant than what the Crown has proven in respect of Mr. Johnston.   

 

[26]           Further, Jason Wisdom had a somewhat more significant criminal record in that he had significant prior sentences for trafficking (three prior PPT convictions) and three prior section 95 convictions.  Jason Wisdom was also out on bail for trafficking when he committed the offence, and again committed subsequent offences prior to conviction on offences for which he was being sentenced.  In contrast, Mr. Johnston has one prior PPT offence and no prior section 95 offences (although he does have prior convictions for possession of a stolen rifle and shotgun).

 

[27]           I accept many of the principles and statements set out by Justice Code in Wilson, for example that 3 to 5 years is the appropriate range for a first section 95 offence where the use and possession of the gun is associated with criminal activity such as drug trafficking (see paragraph 55).  However, I do not accept that Justice Code’s sentencing ranges for commercial wholesale fentanyl trafficking are appropriate for Mr. Johnston based on the proven facts before this Court.  There is a significant difference between the facts in Wisdom and the proven facts in the matter before this Court.  The 13.35 grams of fentanyl mixture possessed by Mr. Johnston is nowhere near the 356.88 grams possessed by Jason Wisdom.  Jason Wisdom received 12.5 years for the possession of the fentanyl (reduced to 9 on totality) but only 5 years concurrent for the possession of the 174.29 grams of powder and crack cocaine.  As indicated, there were many more aggravating factors that applied to Jason Wisdom.  Ultimately it seems to me the 5-year sentence that Jason Wisdom received for the cocaine and 4 years concurrent for the methamphetamine is the better comparator to Mr. Johnston.

 

[28]           The ninth case is R v Fyfe, 2017 SKQB 5 [Fyfe].  Mr. Fyfe sold a fentanyl tablet to someone who overdosed and died as a result. While on bail he sold another two tablets. He plead guilty to the two counts of trafficking in Queen’s Bench. He was 23 with no prior criminal record. He had no real hardship in his youth. He was a low-level dealer and was not completely insightful or remorseful.  The Court imposed a 5-year jail sentence (2.5 years on each count).  Justice Danyliuk set out in that decision a 2-year starting point for low-level dealers of fentanyl (at paragraphs 161-162).

 

[29]           While Fyfe is important as it is dealing with a “low-level” trafficker of fentanyl, it is not a straightforward comparator as you have the aggravating factor of the fact that someone died as a result of Mr. Fyfe’s trafficking, and after that he reoffended again while on bail. There are also no Gladue factors present in that case.  On the other hand, the offender plead guilty, and the charges related to a very low quantity of fentanyl.  Just three pills.   Of further note is that the Saskatchewan Court of Appeal in R v Smith, 2019 SKCA 100, 382 CCC (3d) 455 [Smith], commended the reasoning in Fyfe, but declined to adopt the starting point method for trafficking in fentanyl.

 

[30]           The tenth case is Smith, noted above. I have reviewed the Smith case in detail as it is a leading sentencing case from the Court of Appeal with respect to trafficking in fentanyl, but I note that it is not a good comparator as it deals with a much more high-level situation involving over 1,500 fentanyl pills and related criminal organization and firearm charges.  The sentence was 8 years on the fentanyl trafficking with a global sentence of 13.5 years reduced to 11 on totality. The offender was 32 years old with a much more limited and dated criminal record then Mr. Johnston. What is relevant about Smith is that the Court, in paragraphs 88 through 110, recognized the seriousness and dangers of fentanyl trafficking, the lethality of the drug, and the difficulty in establishing a set range given the relatively recent development of fentanyl epidemics across the country as well as the changing form that fentanyl can take and the associated risks of each. However, at paragraph 132 the Court of Appeal notes that the type of drug (fentanyl) merits significant condemnation.  I will follow that guidance in the sentencing in the matter before me.

 

[31]           The eleventh case is the recent Court of Appeal decision of R v Laird, 2024 SKCA 61 [Laird]. In that case Mr. Laird and his co-accused were driving 310 grams of fentanyl into Saskatchewan to be sold.  The street value was somewhere between $44,000.00 and $124,000.00. They were in possession of five cell phones. They were found guilty after trial. The two men may have been courier drivers from Calgary to Regina.  Each male had a prior conviction for trafficking in a Schedule I substance.  The co-accused, Mr. Mukele also had a prior firearms offence and other serious offences.  Each man was in his 30s and was a first generation Canadian.  The trial judge imposed as sentence of 8 years for each offender for the fentanyl trafficking.  The Court of Appeal upheld the sentence and indicated that the sentences were “on the mark” and not demonstrably unfit.

 

[32]           The Laird decision is a somewhat good comparator however it must be noted that the quantity of fentanyl was substantially higher, and they were found to be mid-level commercial dealers.  As such I find that the same range is not applicable to Mr. Johnston, but I note what the Court of Appeal said at paragraph 145:

 

[145]      I see no basis here to conclude that the sentences imposed against the appellants are demonstrably unfit. As the trial judge recognized, offences related to trafficking in fentanyl rest at the high end of the scale of gravity, and those who commit such offences for commercial gain bear a high level of moral culpability, given that “it is a crime marked by greed and the pursuit  of profit at the expense of violence, death, and the perpetuation of a public health crisis previously unseen in Canadian society … Put simply, it is a crime that can be expected to not only destroy lives, but to undermine the very foundations of our society” (Parranto at para 98, per Moldaver J.). In other words, the offences committed by the appellants were extremely grave. There was nothing in the record to suggest that their moral culpability in committing their offences was significantly attenuated. And, as set out above, the sentence they received was in line with sentences imposed on similar offenders for similar offences committed in similar circumstances. In my view, the sentences were on the mark; they were not demonstrably unfit.

 

[33]           The twelfth and thirteenth cases are R v Felix, 2019 ABCA  458, [2020] 5 WWR 420 and R v Parranto, 2019 ABCA 457, [2020] 5 WWR 1. They were Alberta Court of Appeal decisions in relation to fentanyl trafficking. In both decisions the Alberta Court of Appeal allowed the Crown’s appeal and increased the sentence that the offenders received at trial.  The sentences were 7 years to 10 years imprisonment for Mr. Felix and 11 to 14 years for Mr. Parranto.  Neither case is a particularly relevant comparator because the amounts involved were significantly higher than in the present case and as it dealt with wholesale dealers of fentanyl at the commercial level.

 

[34]           The Court of Appeal sentencing decisions were upheld by the Supreme Court of Canada in R v Parranto, 2021 SCC 46, [2021] 3 SCR 366 [Parranto-SCC] which is the 14th case filed by the Crown. As indicated the trafficking convictions involved significantly more fentanyl, and the offender’s role in the operations were proven to be greater than that of Mr. Johnston in this matter.  Nevertheless, the case is relevant for the statements made by the Court in relation to the seriousness and harm done to the community of trafficking in fentanyl and other hard drugs which goes to the gravity of the offence.  I note that in the partial majority judgment as written by Brown and Martin J.J. at paragraph 70 states:

 

[70]      While not raised by the parties or the court below, this appeal provides an opportunity to emphasize that, when assessing the gravity of the offence, it is open to both the sentencing judge and the Court of Appeal to take into account the offender’s willingness to exploit at‑risk populations and communities. In this regard, choices which demonstrate a reckless disregard for human life increase not only the gravity of the offence but the moral culpability of the offender and may amount to an aggravating factor in sentencing.

 

[35]           I think that is an appropriate comment in the matter before me.  I believe that Mr. Johnston is a mature and intelligent person. He was able to understand the destruction that use of these drugs can have on the lives of other people.  In fact, it is his own methamphetamine use that has significantly affected his own life and is a major factor in what brings him before the Court on these charges, and he recognized the destructive effect of crystal meth in previous pre-sentence reports. I further note the comments about fentanyl trafficking by Moldovar J. in his concurring majority judgment in Parranto-SCC.  I take note of his comments on fentanyl from paragraphs 84 through 94, and at paragraph 98:

 

[98]      The time has thus come for our perception of the gravity of largescale trafficking in fentanyl to accord with the gravity of the crisis it has caused. Largescale trafficking in fentanyl is not a crime marked merely by the distribution and sale of an illicit substance; rather, it is a crime marked by greed and the pursuit of profit at the expense of violence, death, and the perpetuation of a public health crisis previously unseen in Canadian society. In many ways, “[t]rafficking in fentanyl is almost the equivalent of putting multiple bullets in the chambers of a revolver and playing Russian roulette. It is the most efficient killer of drug users on the market today” (R. v. Frazer, 2017 ABPC 116, 58 Alta. L.R. (6th) 185, at para. 11). Put simply, it is a crime that can be expected to not only destroy lives, but to undermine the very foundations of our society.

[36]           It is clear from the guidance in Parranto-SCC that fentanyl trafficking has to be treated as an extremely serious crime. Failing to do so would not give proper effect to the harm caused by the offence and therefore not give proper weight to the gravity of the offence.

 

[37]           The fifteenth case is that of R v Campbell, 2022 ONCA 666, 418 CCC (3d) 279. Mr. Campbell was found by the Court to be a “mid-level” dealer as he was caught by police selling to a low-level dealer.  When arrested at the time of the trafficking transaction he was found in possession of 14.33 grams of heroin laced with fentanyl.  He was found guilty after trial.  He was 41 years old with a lengthy but dated criminal record. He was sentenced to 6 years by the trial judge.  The Ontario Court of Appeal upheld the 6-year sentence as appropriate, noting there were few mitigating factors in the case.

 

[38]           Again, there are some similar factors to the case at hand here, but I note that Mr. Campbell was found to be a “mid-level dealer” selling to low-level dealers.  

 

[39]           The sixteenth case is R v Richer, 2022 ONSC 6872.  Ms. Richer was convicted of trafficking 1 ounce (28 grams) of fentanyl after a jury trial.  Ms. Richer was a first-time offender, she was not the principal dealer in the transaction as she was not high up in the transaction chain.  She had very good prospects for rehabilitation, and she had been doing very well on conditions and had been staying clean and sober.  Yet she continued to lack insight into the gravity of the offence and continued to minimize her actions.  The Court in that case reviewed a number of Ontario decisions and imposed a 4-year sentence of imprisonment.   

 

[40]           I note that in the current case before this Court, Mr. Johnston’s prior criminal record is much more aggravating.

 

[41]           The seventeenth case is that of R v Petrowski, 2020 MBCA 78, 393 CCC (3d) 102. Mr. Petrowski was found guilty after trial of being the principal actor in importing 51 grams of fentanyl powder from China.  The Court accepted the opinion on an expert report that, in such a situation, the fentanyl is commonly “cut at an extremely high ratio” and sold at the 0.1 gram level.  The fentanyl seized in that case could have resulted in 5,100 grams to be sold on the street at a price of $20.00-$50.00 per unit.  The Court took note of further evidence noting the very dangerous and lethal nature of fentanyl.  The Crown in that case argued that the accused was a trafficker that was a “mid-level decision maker with significant trust”.  Mr. Petrowski had a serious youth record but a less serious adult record. The trial Court imposed a 10-year sentence, and it was upheld by the Manitoba Court of Appeal given that Mr. Petrowski was importing fentanyl from China and was using others to insulate himself from detection.

 

[42]           While there are some similarities here, the facts of running a fentanyl importing operation and the evidence of the extremely high street level of sales after being “cut”, make the circumstances in that case somewhat more aggravating than the situation before this Court.

 

[43]           The eighteenth case is that of R v Hurdon, 2023 MBPC 1 [Hurdon].  Mr. Hurdon was found guilty after trial of possessing 8.37 grams of rock fentanyl for the purposes of trafficking as well as $1,575.00 cash proceeds of crime and a small amount of methamphetamine (0.9 grams).  The police had pulled him over driving a stolen vehicle (a charge on which he was acquitted).  Based on the possession of other trafficking paraphernalia (such as cell phones) and the cash on his person, the Court found he possessed the fentanyl for the purpose of trafficking.  He was 32 years old and was selling to support a habit.  His criminal history had been unabated, and he had spent almost all of his teen and adult life in treatment facilities or jail. He had 69 prior convictions including a relatively recent PPT conviction for which he had received 17 months jail and 1 year probation.  He was arrested on the charges just three weeks after his release from jail. However, prior to sentencing he had been in the community for 23 months at a treatment program.  His recent rehabilitative efforts were significant, and he had significant family support. The Court sentenced him to 3.5 years imprisonment.  It noted the sentence was below the 4 to 6 years range the Crown was seeking for street level trafficking, only because of the extraordinary rehabilitative efforts of Mr. Hurdon in the two years prior to sentencing.  

 

[44]           I find the Hurdon is a good comparison to the case at hand in many ways.  For example, lower-level dealer, significant criminal record with one prior PPT conviction, and found guilty after trial. Mr. Johnston urges me to impose the same sentence for the trafficking offences that was imposed in Hurdon.  However, I note Mr. Johnston’s case before the Court is more aggravating due to the high quantity of combined illegal drugs possessed at the time of arrest significantly exceeds that in the Hurdon case.  Mr. Hurdon also had somewhat unusual and significant strides towards rehabilitation in the 23 months before sentencing. These are not insignificant differences, and therefore I find the sentence in Mr. Johnston’s matter should be higher than in the Hurdon case.

 

[45]           The nineteenth case is that of R v Giannelli, 2022 QCCQ 2419 [Giannelli].  Mr. Giannelli was found guilty after trial of possessing the following substances for the purpose of trafficking: 7.25 grams of heroin mixed with fentanyl; 23.64 grams of cocaine; and 12.16 grams of crack cocaine. He was also found to have possessed numerous items indicative of a somewhat sophisticated trafficking operation as well as $14,035.00 in cash.  The Court noted the particular scale of the operation and concluded that this was not a brief lapse in judgment on the part of the accused.  He had a limited criminal record, but it included prior convictions for drug trafficking and criminal organization offences in 2010.  On the other hand, the accused had been doing well on release conditions, had good family support and was gainfully employed.  The Court imposed a sentence of 3.5 years imprisonment.

 

[46]           Again Mr. Johnston asks me to impose a sentence for the trafficking offences similar to the sentence imposed in Giannelli.  While the Court in Giannelli did have evidence of what might be a more significant trafficking operation conducted over a longer time frame, the quantity of drugs possessed were far smaller, the criminal record was not nearly as significant, and the rehabilitative prospects were much better in that case.  While I find it is a helpful comparator, I find there are more aggravating factors in Mr. Johnston’s matter before the Court.

 

[47]           The twentieth case is R v Bellerose, 2019 SKPC 13. Ms. Bellerose plead guilty to possessing 39 grams of meth for the purpose of trafficking.  She was also in possession of $735.00 cash, shotgun shells and the search of her cell phone revealed communications relating to a trafficking operation in which she was found to be a “mid-level dealer” who ran a dial-a-dope business model and sold methamphetamine to street level traffickers.  She was a 42- year-old Métis woman and some Gladue factors were present.  She was previously gainfully employed and had a very limited criminal record (two property offences and two breaches), but she showed minimal remorse.  She was sentenced to 2 years and 3 months. 

 

[48]           Of particular note is Judge Lane’s comments about the dangers of methamphetamine in paragraphs 43 to 50 of his judgment where he comments on the problem of crystal meth in Saskatchewan and how so many offences before the Court seem to be the result of crystal meth addiction. Certainly, those concerns have only increased since 2019 and they remain a concern in Saskatoon.

 

[49]           The last case filed by the Crown was R v Briltz, 2016 SKCA 2, 472 Sask R 201 [Britlz].  Briltz was a case involving a habitual recidivist for numerous different driving offences. The trial court had sentenced Mr. Briltz for numerous offences spanning three separate incidents.  The trial court found a global sentence of 9 years was appropriate but then reduced the sentence to 5 years based on the totality principle.  It gave no reasons for such a significant reduction. The Court of Appeal gave guidance on how to appropriately apply the totality principle and adjusted the reduction in sentence based on totality to a global sentence of 8 years instead of 5 years.

 

[50]           A further case that was not filed, but that I take note of, is that of R v Kidder, 2023 SKKB 185 [Kidder].  The offender in Kidder was the low-level person in a three-person sophisticated meth trafficking operation.  He was essentially just a runner but was aware of the operation. The group was in possession of over five kilograms of crystal meth.  He plead guilty and his criminal record was not extensive, but it was not insignificant. He received a sentence of 5 years for being a “runner” in the organization.

 

[51]           It is not necessarily a good comparator on the facts for a number of reasons, but I mention the case as Justice Elson reviews the dangers and harm done to the community from methamphetamine trafficking in paragraphs 31 through 52.  

 

[52]           I agree with the acknowledgement that methamphetamine truly wreaks devastation in our community.  While fentanyl may have higher rates of overdose deaths then methamphetamine, the addictive nature of crystal meth, its low cost, and its availability in the community mean that it is responsible for so many ruined lives through addiction, destroyed relationships, crimes that are committed as a result of addiction, and services that are required to deal with the number of people suffering and coping with addiction.

 

PERSONAL CIRCUMSTANCES OF THE OFFENDER

[53]           Mr. Johnston is 39 years old.  A PSR that was prepared in relation to this sentencing was filed as Exhibit P-3 on the sentencing outlining many of Mr. Johnston’s personal circumstances. Also filed as Exhibits P-4 and P-5 were PSRs prepared for previous sentencings in November of 2018 and October of 2005 respectively.  They are very helpful in understanding Mr. Johnston’s history.

 

Early Years and Gladue factors

[54]           Mr. Johnston is Indigenous and is a registered band member of Mistawasis First Nation through his mother. His father is registered with Thunderchild First Nation.  Mr. Johnston does not have much connection with those First Nations, and it is unclear if he ever resided there.  He did not have much connection with his father.  His mother had many struggles with the law and with abuse in her home, and as a result Mr. Johnston was in and out of foster care from the age of two. He grew up going in and out of foster care in Regina, Saskatoon, and Prince Albert and sometimes returning to his mother.  He and his family members indicate that he was sexually and physically abused in his youth, inside and outside of foster care.  He faced neglect.  I will not repeat all the details set out in the pre-sentence reports, other than to say that Mr. Johnston has experienced a very difficult life through his youth in foster care and then through the correctional institution.  There is no doubt that the Gladue factors are significant in this matter. There is no doubt that the effects of colonialism have had an impact on his family which in turn played a significant part in the challenges, trauma and hardships he faced as a youth.  It has played a part in his turning to substance use and addiction in his life.  I note that while the significant Gladue factors have been recognized in previous sentencings I continue to recognize them and give effect to them in this matter.

 

[55]           Mr. Johnston does have some good support from his sister and mother, but his struggles with crystal meth mean that he has difficulty utilizing them as a support. He does not stay at his sister’s residence when he is using drugs, and she does not allow his negative peer group at her residence.  Mr. Johnston indicated that, other than his sister, he does not have any peer group that is not involved in drug use or crime. He is a father, but he does not have much involvement in his children’s lives due to his addictions and criminal lifestyle.

 

Addictions and Attitude

[56]           Mr. Johnston indicated his life over the last number of years has been significantly impacted by drug use.  He indicated he has been addicted to crystal meth for approximately 15 years.   His insight into the difficulties he faces seems very good, but his inability to break the cycle of drug use and crime has been a problem. Most of his adult life seems to be a cycle of incarceration, followed by good intentions when released. Shortly after release, he falls back into use of crystal meth and then a cycle of crime resulting from use of meth, or as a need to support himself financially. He does seem to have some very good insight on the dangers and destructiveness of crystal meth use.  He explains that crystal meth is the worst drug because it is so addictive, cheap and readily available. He says of crystal meth: “the other ones you can do a bit and go back to your normal life but this one you get hooked, get ringed in, and you’re done”.  I find that Mr. Johnston does have some insight and remorse for his criminal offending but at this time lacks an ability to break the cycle of addiction and crime that he falls into when released from jail.  Without a plan, good supports, and without making significant progress on his addiction to crystal meth, there is no reason to think that he will not continue to offend when released again. The PSR indicates he is a high risk to reoffend.  

 

Employment

[57]           Mr. Johnston has not had stable employment for some time. He does strike me as quite an intelligent person.  He has the skills and ability to make a change if he can remove himself from the drug and criminal lifestyle.

 

Criminal Record

[58]           Unfortunately, Mr. Johnston is 39 years old and has compiled quite a criminal record. The Crown filed a copy of his record and transcripts of 13 of his prior sentencings at the sentencing in this matter. Mr. Johnston has approximately 94 prior convictions (22 of those as a youth) and a continuing history of weapon, drug, property, and driving offences.  He has numerous convictions for noncompliance with court orders.

 

[59]           Mr. Johnston does have a number of violent offences on his record such as assaults and robbery, but as his counsel points out, he has no convictions for violent offences since 2011.   He was sentenced to a 26-month penitentiary sentence in 2005 for a number of serious assaults and violated his statutory release.  Otherwise, he has not received a penitentiary sentence.

 

[60]           Having said that, it is noted that Mr. Johnston has spent little time in the community in the last 10 years. He has numerous sentences involving correctional time for serious driving offences and other property or weapon offences.  In 2016 he was sentenced to 2 years less a day for flight from police, dangerous operation of a vehicle, possession of stolen rifle and shotgun, and numerous other charges. He was again sentenced to 2 years less a day in 2020 for driving offences and drug possession.  In 2021 he was sentenced again to 2 years less a day for trafficking in fentanyl (possession of 37 grams), driving while prohibited, carrying concealed bear spray and proceeds of crime. I note he also had pre-sentence custody of 189 days jail meaning it was approximately a 2.5-year global sentence.  To date, that has been his longest jail sentence. However, he has received numerous sentences around that 2-year range.

 

STATUTORY CONSIDERATIONS

[61]           I take note of the purposes and principle of sentencing as set out in the Criminal Code under section 718 as well as section 10 of the CDSA. In particular, the fundamental principle of sentencing set out in section 718.1, the principle of parity under 718.2(b), and the principle of restraint under section 718.2(e).

 

[62]           Given the nature of the offences, the case law I have reviewed, and Mr. Johnston’s criminal record. I find the primary objective to be applied in this sentencing is protection of the public and denunciation and deterrence, which can only be achieved by separating Mr. Johnston from society for an appropriate period of time.

 

AGGRAVATING FACTORS

[63]           Criminal Record - As noted earlier the prior criminal record of Mr. Johnston is very aggravating.  Mr. Johnston has a record of seemingly continuous offending.  I agree with the Crown that this is a situation similar to that in Ratt where the situation cries out for a sentence that puts protection of the pubic at the forefront though the objective of separating Mr. Johnston from the community for a period of time.  Counsel for Mr. Johnston argues that this is different from Ratt as Mr. Ratt had a history of “unstopped violence” and Mr. Johnston does not have that.  However, Mr. Johnston’s recent record does show a history of behavior which puts the public at considerable risk, be it through significant driving offences, trafficking in deadly and destructive drugs, or possession of weapons and firearms in connection with criminality.  I note that in the case law reviewed earlier, there were few offenders with such significant records.  

 

[64]           The combination of drugs that were possessed by Mr. Johnston – I note the combination of the possession of methamphetamine, fentanyl, cocaine and GHB along with the other drugs is aggravating.  This is not just a dealer possessing just 114 grams of meth or just 13.35 grams of fentanyl. The sentence for the drug counts must reflect the “total gravity” of this joint meth, fentanyl, cocaine and GHB operation.  I also accept that, in the cases set out above, courts have recognized that the dangers of the fentanyl mixture, such as was present in this case, can be aggravating. I take note of the Saskatchewan Coroners Service report and Affidavit of Graham Jones filed by the Crown as to the dangers of fentanyl to the community. 

 

MITIGATING FACTORS

[65]           Mr. Johnston’s Gladue factors – While not technically a mitigating factor that automatically reduces the sentence, Gladue factors can factor into the assessment of the degree of responsibility of the offender and be applied through the principle of restraint under section 718.2(e).  I note that I do give the Gladue factors some weight throughout the sentencing process for Mr. Johnston given the role they played in shaping the challenges of Mr. Johnston’s youth which played a part in his addictions, attitude, and pro-criminal associations as an adult. His life has been a cycle of addictions and criminal involvement that has been difficult to break.  The Gladue factors do bear on the degree of responsibility of the offender and as set out in the case of R v Chanalquay, 2015 SKCA 141, 472 Sask R 110 the question is: what is the appropriate sentence for this offence, committed by this offender, harming this community? Having said that, I further note that Gladue factors do not result in an automatic reduction of sentence. Given his age, criminal history, sentencing history, and the serious criminal charges before the Court the Gladue factors affect the sentence to a somewhat lesser degree than if a community-based sentence or a correctional sentence were an option.  Just as was the situation in Ratt, I am going to apply the Gladue factors to my sentencing analysis throughout, but in this case I find that they do not displace or override the primary sentencing objective in this matter, which is the need to separate Mr. Johnston from the public for a period of time to achieve the primary purpose of this sentencing, which is protection of the public, as well as the objectives of denunciation and deterrence for these serious crimes.  Just as in Ratt, I note that Gladue factors have been applied in previous sentencings for Mr. Johnston, with the promise to change, but that change has not been forthcoming.  This Court is not giving up on the objective of rehabilitation, but the question will be, what length of detention is necessary to reasonably conclude that his danger to society has subsided, and what sentence is sufficient to give the correctional authorities the necessary time to properly treat Mr. Johnston and assess his risk.

 

[66]           Some pro-social supports – Mr. Johnston does have the support of his sister and mother, although he has had difficulty utilizing that support in the community on his own.  

 

[67]           There are not many other mitigating factors, Mr. Johnston does not have the benefit of a guilty plea for most of his matters. The gravity of the offences is high, and the degree of responsibility is also high as Mr. Johnston knows the risks and the impacts of these offences (although his degree of responsibility is somewhat impacted by the Gladue factors that I have mentioned).  

 

ANALYSIS AND SENTENCE

[68]           I now turn to the analysis of the appropriate sentence.

 

The drug offences - counts 1 through 4

[69]           As I indicated earlier the sentence should reflect the “total gravity” of the combined level of drugs possessed for the purpose of trafficking.  I have gone through the numerous decisions as set out above, and I find that the Crown’s position of 7 years is too high.  Specifically in this situation Mr. Johnston did not possess a level of fentanyl that compares to many of the cases that the Crown relies on.  Further, in this particular case, I do not find that the Crown has proven that Mr. Johnston was a “mid-level or commercial dealer”.  As set out in Smith at paragraph 46, in sentencing an offender it is important to know how significant an offender’s involvement in the drug trade and criminal activity was. The Court noted the lines demarking these categories are not always clear. The categories that seem to emerge from the case law are concepts such as “low-level dealer”, be it a street level dealer or dial-a-dope dealer, a courier, a “mid-level dealer” selling to street level dealers, or “high-level” dealers or importers. It is also important to remember there exists no bright lines on many of these categories, and individuals involved in the drug trade may move between them. Low-level dealers may sell product to persons that only sell to one or two other users.  The facts and circumstances with respect to offences may support competing or different inferences as to whether a person was a “mid level” or “low-level” dealer. Even within each category, there can be a significant degree of variation in involvement and scale of the drug trade. For example, there can be significant differences between a street level dealer in possession of 20 grams of crystal meth on a street corner selling at the point level, simply to feed an addiction for very little profit, and a street dealer who was running a dial-a-dope operation involving far larger quantities of crystal meth and other drugs for which they are selling considerably more product to more people, most of whom are generally end users, but some of whom may further distribute small amounts. I note it seems that difficulty in dealing with the wide variety of circumstances that come before the Court is why the Saskatchewan Court of Appeal in Smith expressed some reservation, and noted the problems, in establishing a hard and fast starting point for fentanyl trafficking. The Court of Appeal recognized the significant extent to which sentencing is an individualized process.  

 

[70]           In this particular case, I accept the opinion of the Crown drug expert, Cst. Kennedy, that the amount of drugs, particularly crystal meth, possessed by Mr. Johnston could be consistent with a mid-level dealer. Having said that, the expert evidence could not provide a definitive answer or characterization to that effect and found it was also consistent with a lower level or street dealer. In this particular case, as opposed to many of the other cases that the Crown relies on in their submissions, there is no evidence of trafficking transactions from a cell phone search, from prior surveillance, or from other trafficking paraphernalia such as accounting records or score sheets. In addition, there is no substantial amount of cash present. The amount of cash on Mr. Campodonico would be consistent with a low-level dealer. There is no evidence of supplying product to another dealer, or of how Mr. Johnston is an integral part of a larger drug operation.

 

[71]           As a result, I do not find that the Crown has proven that Mr. Johnston is a mid-level dealer or that he is anything other than intending to sell his product at the street level. There is no evidence of substantial profit. I accept that Mr. Johnston was selling drugs both to support his habit and for a level of financial gain. Having said that, I do find the number of drugs possessed for trafficking by Mr. Johnston to be on the high end for a low-level or street-level dealer. 

 

[72]           Given the primary sentencing purposes and objectives in this matter that I have previously set out, and given Mr. Johnston’s prior criminal record (which is significant), specifically his prior conviction for possessing fentanyl for the purpose of trafficking, the combined level of drugs present in this case (which is also aggravating), and the fact that he does not have the mitigating factor of a guilty plea as he did on his prior PPT sentencing, I find that the appropriate sentence for the drug offences is 5 years imprisonment applied as follows:

 

a.                  5 years on count 1 (fentanyl);

 

b.                  5 years concurrent on count 2 (meth);

 

c.                  3 years concurrent on count 3 (GHB);

 

d.                  4 years concurrent on count 4 (cocaine).

 

[73]           The firearm offences – I now turn to the firearm offences.  As suggested by the Crown, I will enter a stay of proceedings on counts 7 and 8 as those offences are stayed pursuant to Kienapple[3]. With respect to the section 95 offence in count 5, I find that the sentence must be at least 3 years as I find the firearm was possessed in a situation of criminality in relation to the drug trade. The firearm itself is a .22 caliber semi-automatic rifle.  It is a prohibited weapon (possibly because it looks like an assault style rifle although I cannot be sure of that).  There is no indication it was altered in any way. It was not loaded, although I did find the ammunition was readily accessible, however as noted by the defence counsel the weapon itself was stored in the trunk of the vehicle in a way in which it was not easily accessed.  If it is considered to be possessed “in public” it must be one of the lesser degrees of “in public” possession you could have.  For example, it would be an entirely different situation if Mr. Johnston were to have had it loaded and concealed on his person outside of a community centre such as in Nur. That would be far more aggravating.

 

[74]           The firearm in question here was not easily concealed on a person or stored directly with the drugs, such as a loaded handgun or sawed-off shotgun as noted in other cases. Furthermore, unlike some of the other cases filed, I am not able to make a finding on a reasonable inference about how Mr. Johnston came to be connected with the firearm in the trunk.  I found beyond a reasonable doubt that he had knowledge and control of it, but I do not know the circumstances as to how it came into his possession. Did he purchase it for the purposes of the drug trade or was it simply provided to him along with the drugs he was to sell?

 

[75]           Under all the circumstances, I find the possession of the firearm in violation of section 95, although very serious, not as aggravating as in some of the other cases noted. I further note that Mr. Johnston has no prior section 95 conviction.  He does have a prior firearms conviction for unlawful possession of a standard rifle and shotgun, but the situation is nowhere near as aggravating as situation set out in Wisdom.  I do, however, accept that under the circumstances the sentence should be consecutive to the other sentences imposed.

 

[76]           Under all the circumstances I find that, in this particular case, for this offender, the appropriate sentence under section 95 for count 5 on the information is 3 years consecutive to all other counts.

 

[77]           With respect to count 6 (the section 94 offence) I impose as sentence of 2 years concurrent to count 5.

 

[78]           Section 117 offences – With respect to the section 117.01 offences under counts 9 and 10, I find the appropriate sentence should be 1 year imprisonment on each, concurrent but consecutive to the sentences on all other counts.  I realize the Crown is asking for the sentences to be consecutive to each other, but in all the circumstances, and given that I am imposing the 1-year sentence for each, I find it is appropriate that they are concurrent.  I believe that properly reflects the gravity of the offence and the degree of responsibility of the offender in this situation.  

 

[79]           The driving while prohibited offence – I note Mr. Johnston has a terrible record for driving offences and numerous prior drive-while prohibited convictions.  He did enter a guilty plea for this offence on the trial date.  The guilty plea is mitigating, but not to a significant degree in this case. When sentenced for prior drive while prohibited offences the sentence has been in the range of 6 to 8 months when it was applied as a consecutive sentence.  I think the appropriate sentence on count 11 under all the circumstances is 10 months consecutive to all other sentences on all other counts.

 

[80]           Totality – The combined sentence is a global sentence of 9 years and 10 months for Mr. Johnston.  Pursuant to section 718.2(c), I must now examine the combined sentence to determine if it is unduly long or harsh.  Pursuant to Briltz, if I reduce the sentence I must explain why, and the adjusted sentence must still be consistent with the fundamental sentencing principal of proportionality set out in section 718.1. 

 

[81]           In looking at the combined global sentence, I do believe the sentence is unduly long, and some reduction based on the totality principle is appropriate in this case for the following reasons:

 

1.      The global sentence in this case results from a single vehicle stop on one night and not a series of offences over a wide-ranging time span that have been brought together for a global sentencing. The consecutive sentences arise from many different counts which relate to the different legal interests at stake rather than different actual victims. They result from a poor decision on the part of Mr. Johnston to possess the drugs for the purpose of trafficking on that day. This is not a situation where Mr. Johnston committed offences and then committed further offences while on bail at different periods of time with a consolidated sentencing process for his benefit.

 

2.      The longest sentence Mr. Johnston had received previously was for 2 and ½ years and it was for a very similar type of offence, although I acknowledge it did not include the significantly more serious factor of possession of the prohibited firearm, with readily accessible ammunition, and corresponding section 117 charges.  Certainly, the combined amount of drugs is greater in this situation than his last trafficking conviction, but it nevertheless gives me some concern that the sentence on the trafficking and driving offences may not be in line with the “step principle” without some reduction in sentence based on totality.

 

3.      As I have indicated I do not completely give up hope of any rehabilitation with respect to Mr. Johnston and I can apply the Gladue principles under section 718.2 (e) at this stage of analysis. I find that an 8 and ½ year global sentence would still not compromise public safety and would still allow there to be sufficient time to give correctional authorities the necessary time to properly treat Mr. Johnston and to assess his risk for reoffending. I also find that a global sentence of 8 and ½ years would still give proper effect to the overall gravity of the combined offence and the degree of responsibility of the offender. It would also give effect to the principle of restraint with respect to Indigenous offenders that section 718.2 (e) was designed to deal with.

 

[82]           Therefore, on the basis of totality, I am adjusting the sentence to reflect a global sentence of 8.5 years rather than 9 years and 10 months:

 

a.      I am going to make the sentence on count 11 concurrent to counts 9 and 10;

b.      I am going to reduce the sentence on counts 1 and 2 by 6 months so that the 5-year sentence is now 4.5 years on each count.

 

[83]           Remand credit – I will now adjust the sentence going forward to give effect to Mr. Johnston’s pre-sentence custody remand credit. He has been in custody on these charges since July 23, 2023.  That is 473 days actual credit enhanced at 1.5 to 710 days presentence custody credit.   I will apply that credit to the sentences on counts 1 through 4.

 

[84]           The sentence on counts will be as follows:

 

a.      Count 1 - 1,642 days less 710 days = 932 days to serve going forward;

b.      Count 2 - 1,642 days less 710 days = 932 days to serve concurrent to count 1;

c.      Count 3 - 1,095 days less 710 days = 385 days concurrent to count 1 and 2;

d.      Count 4 - 1,460 days less 710 = 750 days concurrent to 1, 2, 3;

e.      Count 5 - 1,095 days - consecutive to counts 1 thought 4;

f.        Count 6 - 730 days - concurrent to count 5 but consecutive to all other counts;

g.      Count 9 - 365 days - concurrent to counts 10 and 11 but consecutive to all other counts;

h.      Count 10 - 365 days - concurrent to counts 9 and 11 but consecutive to all other counts;

i.        Count 11 - 304 days – concurrent to counts 9 and 10 but consecutive to all other counts.

 

[85]           Going forward, the intention is that Mr. Johnston have 2,392 days to serve going forward, which would be just over 6 and ½ years remaining.   

 

ANCILLARY ORDERS

[86]           I will also make the following ancillary orders:

 

a.      On counts 1-5 – lifetime firearms prohibition under section 109;

b.      On counts 1-5 – secondary DNA order under section 487.051;

c.      On count 11 – a 2-year driving prohibition on your release from custody under section 320.24.

 

[87]           I will waive the victim fine surcharge on all counts due to financial hardship.
